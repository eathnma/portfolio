Up Top!

Up Top! is a web experience that lets you high-five your coworkers virtually. Using hand tracking software, the project aims to provide remote teams with a playful way to celebrate the small wins in their workday.

Context

Up Top! is a playful exploration of how hand-gesture greetings could be reimagined online using computer vision and peer-to-peer web technology. Prototyped as a website, we envision the interaction as a built-in feature of a video-calling platform, such as Zoom or Google Meet.

This project was created in the context of a directed study, focusing on exploring social interactions for the remote workplace through the creative code medium.

“How might we reduce social isolation during COVID-19 by creating an informal way for remote workers to connect throughout the workday?

HOW TO USE UP-TOP!

Step 1: Enter the Room

When users open Up Top!, they are first asked to enter a name to let others know who's there. To invite a friend or coworker to the room, users can share a link to the website.

Step 2: Raise your hand

For our prototype, we used Handtrack.js to detect the user's hand movements in the webcam. The hand’s position is then tracked and mapped to the hand on screen.

Step 3: Go for the High Five!

Once two people are in the room, users can move their hands towards each other to give a high five. The website will respond by loading a random GIF and will play the song "Celebration" for as long as the two hands are touching.

User-Testing

As this was an experimental interaction, we were unsure how remote-workers would react. To get feedback on the interaction, we tested the prototype with a small group, including four product designers, one game developer, and an HR assistant. Here are our main takeaways:

Too intimate for work

While the virtual high-five feels more personal than a "good job," half of our users found the experience too intimate for work. One participant explained that because they and their high-five partner had to hold their hands in place in order to see the celebratory feedback, the interaction felt more like a handhold than a traditional high five.

“ We’re just gently caressing each other’s hands.”

Inefficient, but intentional

The high five would be work-appropriate only if it were more efficient, citing awkwardness and time as reasons not to use the current interaction. However, users liked that the interaction challenged them to be more intentional about their movements. These users felt that Up Top! would be a fun experience to share at a work social.

"If it worked smoothly, I could see myself using it at work on team calls or one-on-ones."

Fun with embodied interactions

Overall, users agreed that the current interaction may not be appropriate for a busy workday, but it was a fun and novel interaction nonetheless. In this way, the virtual high-five was successful in sparking joy and helping users feel more connected to the person on the other side.

"I don't know if this would be efficient for my workplace but it’s fun."

Moving Forward

Oftentimes, remote work feels stressful and rushed, with little time for socialization. With our interaction, we aimed to create an activity that people could bond over, making online work feel less isolating.

Through the user-interviews, we think our interaction is not workplace ready, but we are thrilled that remote-workers expressed enthusiasm in our experiment, and would see themselves using this within the workday. To make this feasible, there are interaction and technical aspects that need to be revisited, such as improving the intentionality of the high five, as well as boosting the performance of the application. Overall, because we received such positive feedback, this experiment was a success in our books.
