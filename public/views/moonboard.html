Moonboard - A Redesign for a Mobile Climbing App

Our interaction and visual redesign of the Moonboard mobile app aims to facilitate problem discovery, enrich training, and build a community to help advanced climbers train alongside a global community.

What is a Moonboard?

Each Moonboard has identical LED holds and connects to a mobile app, which gives users the ability to browse problems, log their completed climbs, and compare their performance against friends and professional athletes. Each route is set and reviewed by users on the app to determine a mutually agreed difficulty level.

From amateur to world-class, a global community of climbers use the Moonboard to train their strength and power endurance.

Problem

When the Moonboard was first released, the training apparatus dominated the climbing scene. However, due to the recent growth of the sport, other types of training boards such as Kilterboard and Tension Board have started to overtake Moonboard in social popularity. This is due to Kilter and Tension's strong social media presence, with climbers having the ability to upload and view videos on the board‚Äôs mobile apps.

Compared to the other training boards, Moonboard‚Äôs mobile app is outdated, with new and experienced climbers even struggling to navigate the basics of the app. Without the core functionality working, Moonboard cannot even begin to compete in social media against its competitors.

From insights pulled from our user interviews, we built an experience design strategy around three key objectives: facilitating problem discovery, enriching training, and building community. The redesigned app features a simplified navigation and filtering system based on feedback from new and experienced users. SOLUTION

How the Moonboard Works

When climbers turn on the board, they are presented with an onboarding screen showing how to swipe through the climbs. With each swipe on the digital app, the lights on the physical board will change. Climbers can scroll down for additional information that helps them choose a climb suitable to their skill level.

Improvements

1. In the original design, there was no affordance for the swiping interaction. To help with this, we created a small onboarding screen and added an animation to show users that they could swipe through climbs.

2. We also rehauled sections of the information regarding the climbs, as the visualizations presented in the original design did not benefit the user. We polished it up by only including the bare essentials needed to judge the quality of a climb, reducing cognitive overhead.

Tap to Save!

When climbers find a climb but cannot complete them, they can save it to a climbing list for another session. Each climbing list has attributes such as climbs completed, grade range, as well as list collaborators.

Improvements

1. In a user interview, a climber mentioned that saving and accessing climbs in the original app was difficult due to the interaction design. We decided to simplify this by following current interaction design patterns by moving the ‚Äúsave‚Äù button to the top right.

2. In the original app, climbers have two choices when clicking on the ‚Äú+‚Äù icon at the bottom of the navigation. Climbers can save a climb to their list or logbook. To a new user, saving climbs to a list or logbook can mean the same thing, which guided the redesign to separate them into different buttons.

Completing Climbs

After completing a climb, climbers log them with a short form to keep track of their progress. After completing the form, a small celebration appears and prompts the climber to upload a video to the community. The green checkmark beside the climb‚Äôs name signifies the completion of the route.

Improvements

1. Completing a climb on the Moonboard is extremely difficult, and it is common for climbers to celebrate afterward. There is an opportunity for the digital experience to match the enthusiasm of finishing a climb on the Moonboard. Because of this, we designed the interactions to feel joyful and springy, with congratulations at the end.

2. After a climb, it is most likely that the climber‚Äôs fingers are weak. We decided to blow up the buttons to make logging more accessible to climbers with tired fingers.

Cleaning up the Filtering System

In a user interview we discovered that climbers want a simplified method to filter climbs based on three main determinants:

Benchmarked Climbs - Is the climb verified by the MoonBoard community?
Completion Number - Is it even possible to climb?
Star Rating - How well does the climb combine strength and technique?

Our new filtering page also auto converts between Font or V grading. The Font scale is commonly used in Europe, and the V scale is used in North America to grade climbs. This way, climbers don‚Äôt have to convert between the two systems.
