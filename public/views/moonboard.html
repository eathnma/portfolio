Context

I created this project with Audrey Chow, as a exercise to improve in interaction design. Our project focuses on aligning and opening up opportunities for elite climbers to access the board. This interaction design project was completed as a four week sprint, with an additional two weeks to refine and polish the prototype.

What is a Moonboard?

Each wall is set up with identical LED holds and connects to a mobile app, which gives users the ability to browse problems, log their completed climbs, and compare their performance against friends and professional athletes. All of the problems are set by users on the app and reviewed by other climbers for a mutually agreed difficulty level.

From amateur to world-class, a global community of climbers use the Moonboard to train their strength and power endurance.

SOLUTION

How the Moonboard Works

When climbers turn on the board, they are greeted with an onboarding screen that shows them how to swipe through the climbs. With each swipe on the digital app, the lights on the physical board will change. Climbers can scroll down for additional information that helps them choose a climb suitable to their skill level.

Improvements

1. In the original design, there was no affordance for the swiping interaction. To help with this, we created a small onboarding screen and added an animation to show users that they were able to swipe.

2. We also rehauled sections of the information regarding the climbs, as the visualizations presented in the original design did not benefit the user. We polished it up by only including the bare essentials needed to judge the quality of a climb, reducing cognitive overhead.

Tap to Save!

When climbers find a climb but are unable to finish it, they can save it to a climbing list for another session. Each climbing list has attributes such as numbers of climbs completed, grade range, as well as list collaborators.

Improvements

1. In a user interview, a climber mentioned that saving and accessing climbs in the original app was difficult, due to the interaction design. We decided to simplify this by following current interaction design patterns, by moving the “save” button to the top right.

2. In the original app, climbers have two choices when clicking on the “+” icon at the bottom of the navigation. Climbers either can save a climb to their list or logbook. To a new user, the distinction between the two is confusing, hence our decision to separate the two into two different buttons.

Completing Climbs

After successfully completing a climb, climbers log their climbs with a simple form to keep track of their progress. After completing the form, a small celebration appears and prompts the climber to upload a video to the community. The green checkmark beside the climb’s name signifies the completion of the climb.

Improvements

1. Completing a climb on the Moonboard is extremely difficult, and it is common for climbers to celebrate afterward. There is an opportunity for the digital experience to match the enthusiasm of finishing a climb on the Moonboard. Because of this, we designed the interactions to feel joyful and springy, with congratulations at the end.

2. After a hard climb, it is most likely that the climber’s fingers are quite weak. We decided to blow up the buttons to make logging easier, especially with tired fingers.

Cleaning up the Filtering System

In a user interview we discovered that climbers want a simplified method to filter climbs based on three main determinants:

Benchmarked Climbs - Is the climb verified by the MoonBoard community?
Completion Number - Is it even possible to climb?
Star Rating - How well does the climb combine strength and technique?

Our new filtering page also auto converts between Font or V grading. The Font scale is commonly used in Europe, and the V scale is used in North America to grade climbs. This way, climbers don’t have to convert between the two systems.
